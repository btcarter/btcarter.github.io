Purpose

This tutorial features a discussion of neuroimaging atlases and their uses to facilitate choosing an appropriate atlas for a neuroimaging project.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this tutorial, you should be able to meet the following objectives:

  1. Understand what a structural atlas is.
  2. Describe why they are used.
  3. Describe how to choose an appropriate atlas for imaging.
  4. Know common atlases for specific study populations.
  5. Download the ICBM152 atlas in MNI space.
  6. Download the OASIS-20-TRT volumes and labels for parcellation.

What are they?

Atlases are 3-dimensional maps of the brain that use a standardized anatomy and coordinate system. This allows researchers to make comparisons between experiments and report findings using coordinates rather than being limited to just a structure, allowing for a more refined measure of location. Coordinate systems for atlases are predefined and termed spaces. There are two predominant coordinate systems, Talairach space and MNI space. Talairach space is the older of the two and was originally developed in the 1960's for use in deep brain surgery. The system was created via histological sectioning of a single brain. As a result there is some inconsistency in the system due to an n of 1. MNI space was developed more recently using statistical maps to create an "idealized" brain upon which the coordinate system was based. Of these two systems, MNI space is one of the most widely used in the current literature. A number of atlases have been developed based on this system including the MNI305, Colin27, ICBM152, and ICBM452 atlases. These atlases represent whole brain atlases. Other atlases have been developed for specific domains of research such as pediatric studies, surface feature specific atlases, white matter atlases, and regional atlases (such as for the hippocampus).

How do I choose one?

Atlases should be chosen based on the study population and hypothesis. Whole brain studies of adults should utilize a whole brain atlas such as the MNI152 atlas. Pediatric studies should utilize a pediatric atlas. Studies of specific features such as white matter should use white matter specific atlases. Disease specific atlases are also being developed for conditions such as Alzheimer's Disease and multiple sclerosis.

Our Project

For the purposes of this tutorial we are going to examine a population of adults without known neurological abnormality. Therefore a whole brain atlas is appropriate (in this case the ICBM152 atlas).

Get the ICBM152 atlas

Once you have an account, you should log into it. This is done via the terminal using the command ssh. This stands for secure shell client.

Once you have entered the command the terminal should prompt you for a password. Enter it. Notice that the cursor will not move as you enter your password. You should see something like this after logging on successfully.

Make directory called atlases using the mkdir command, then switch into that directory. Then make a directory labeled mni

Use the command wget to download the ICBM 152 atlas into your atlases directory.

Next unzip the file with the command unzip.

Parcellation

Parcellation refers to dividing the brain into separate regions. This is generally done based on either functional or anatomical features of the brain. Manual methods (when a trained scientist personally labels brain structures by hand) are the gold standard for accurate parcellation. However these methods are tedious and time consuming as they require neuroimagers to label the brain slice by slice. When data sets get large this becomes problematic and slows the rate of computation. Reasonable and accurate automated methods have been developed to fill this need. In order to use this method, however, we need to download some pre-labeled atlases to use as a reference.

Get the OASIS-TRT-20 Volumes and Labels

Next you will need to get a labeling atlas that you could use later. Download the OASIS-TRT-20_volumes.zip from Mindboggle101_release2 on the Open Science Foundation website. Navigate to https://osf.io/7dwfc/ and download the zip file into your Downloads folder. Upload the zip file to your atlases directory on the supercomputer using the command rsync.

Next download the individual labels to your local Downloads directory. Navigate to https://osf.io/yhkde/files/. Under the whole_brains_labels_OASIS-TRT-20 tab select the OASIS-TRT-20_DKT31_CMA_labels_v2 by clicking it once so it is highlighted. Click the Download button in the top right of the page. Upload this file to the atlases directory of your supercomputer account.
